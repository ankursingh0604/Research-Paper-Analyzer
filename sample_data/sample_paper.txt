Title: Sparse Windowed Attention for Efficient Long-Context Summarization

Authors: R. Menon, T. Okafor, S. Lindqvist
Venue: Workshop on Efficient NLP Systems, 2025

Abstract
Transformer-based summarization models scale quadratically with input length,
making them expensive to run on long documents such as research papers or
legal contracts. We introduce Sparse Windowed Attention (SWA), an attention
variant that restricts each token to attend only to a local window plus a
small set of learned "anchor" tokens that summarize distant context. On a
held-out set of 1,200 long-form documents, SWA reduces attention FLOPs by
71% relative to full self-attention while retaining 96% of ROUGE-L score
compared to a full-attention baseline of equal parameter count. We further
show that SWA's anchor tokens can be visualized to reveal which passages the
model treats as globally important, providing a useful interpretability
signal that dense attention does not offer.

1. Introduction
Long documents are common in scientific, legal, and enterprise settings, yet
most transformer summarizers are trained and evaluated on inputs under 1,024
tokens due to the quadratic cost of self-attention. Prior long-context
approaches (Longformer, BigBird) use fixed sparsity patterns but discard the
ability to dynamically decide which distant tokens matter for a given
document. We propose SWA to address this gap.

2. Method
SWA combines two attention pathways per layer: (a) a local sliding window of
size w=256 tokens, and (b) attention to a small set of k=32 learned anchor
tokens, updated via a lightweight pooling network that compresses each
1,024-token block into a single anchor vector. This keeps attention cost
linear in sequence length while allowing information to flow across the full
document through the anchor pathway. We initialize SWA from a pretrained
encoder-decoder checkpoint and fine-tune on document-summary pairs.

3. Experiments
We evaluate on three long-document summarization benchmarks (arXiv papers,
GovReport, and PubMed) against a full-attention baseline and two sparse
baselines (Longformer, BigBird) of matched parameter count. We measure
ROUGE-1/2/L, training throughput (tokens/sec), and peak memory usage.

4. Results
SWA matches full attention within 1-4 ROUGE-L points across all three
benchmarks while using 71% fewer attention FLOPs and 2.3x higher training
throughput. Compared to Longformer and BigBird at equal sparsity budget, SWA
improves ROUGE-L by 2.1 and 3.4 points respectively, which we attribute to
the learned (rather than fixed) anchor mechanism. Ablations show that
removing the anchor pathway entirely (local window only) drops ROUGE-L by
8.7 points, confirming that global context access is necessary for
document-level coherence.

5. Related Work
Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020) introduced a mix of local windowed
attention and fixed global tokens. BigBird (Zaheer et al., 2020) combined
windowed, global, and random attention to approximate full attention while
retaining theoretical expressiveness guarantees. Reformer (Kitaev et al.,
2020) used locality-sensitive hashing to approximate attention. Our anchor
pooling mechanism is related to Perceiver's (Jaegle et al., 2021) latent
bottleneck but is applied per-block rather than globally, which we show
preserves more fine-grained positional signal for summarization.

6. Conclusion
SWA offers a practical middle ground between full and fixed-sparsity
attention for long-document summarization, with learned anchors providing
both efficiency gains and a lightweight interpretability signal. Future work
includes extending SWA to multi-document summarization and exploring
adaptive window sizes per layer.
